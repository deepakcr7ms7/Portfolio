My Journey

The long road that shaped me.

Not everything is on the resume. Here's the story that got me here.

The Dream Begins

I didn’t grow up planning to study abroad. The idea arrived quietly and stayed. I wanted something harder. Something that would push me beyond what felt comfortable. In a family where my siblings were accomplished doctors, I knew I had to carve my own space.

“I knew I needed to do something special to even stand near them.”

Choosing My Path

Engineering felt natural. I liked breaking things down, understanding how systems worked, and building something from nothing. Still, the question followed me everywhere: Why not become a doctor?

“Every time I answer that question, I pause. I’ve doubted it before — but this path felt honest.”

The Admit Letter

During exam prep, campus placements were happening. Friends were interviewing, getting offers, celebrating. I stayed focused on a different goal. Rejections came first — then one day, an email arrived.

I was sitting with friends when I saw it. My first admit letter. Everyone noticed. Everyone celebrated. For the first time, the dream felt real.

“Among all that noise, I had to stay true to my goal.”

The Storm

I thought the hard part was over. Then COVID hit. My 2020 plans collapsed overnight. Everything was deferred. The waiting stretched on, filled with uncertainty, paperwork, and anxiety.

In that chaos, I injured my hand — something I’d carry into my first year. Pain became background noise.

“Three bags. Twenty-three years. One flight.”

First Flight, First Landing

I had never flown before. Not even once. My first flight wasn’t for vacation — it was to the U.S. Embassy in Delhi. That’s when it hit me: this was really happening.

I cried once before leaving. Not in front of my mom. I carried everything quietly.

New Country, New Pressure

The U.S. felt overwhelming at first. New food. New weather. Speaking English all day took effort. I overloaded credits, worked as a TA, and tried to save money wherever I could.

Everyone talked about internships. I didn’t get one. Summer came — and I stayed back alone in the dorms.

“No internship. Just silence in the dorms.”

Borrowed Time

Graduation came with no job offer — only a ticking clock. I had 90 days. When I narrowly missed a required grade in my ML specialization, I thought another door had closed.

Instead, a professor offered me a Research Assistant role. It didn’t solve everything — but it gave me time.

“That one offer gave me time. And I needed all of it.”

Survival Mode

Money ran out. Rejections kept coming. I biked through winter to a gas station job. Later, I worked at Subway.

Those jobs didn’t define me — but the people did. Small kindnesses helped me stay grounded when confidence was gone.

“One month of survival. That was the goal.”

The Call

Ten months after graduation, another interview. I prepared quietly, without expectations. A coding round caught me off guard — but I relied on work I’d done long ago.

An hour after the interview, my phone rang.

“We’d like to offer you the job.”

The Breakthrough

I cried — not because of the job, but because of everything it took to get there. I called my mom. She didn’t need words.

It wasn’t luck. It was persistence.

“If you keep going — even when it’s quiet, even when it hurts — you’ll make it.”

Now

I’ve come a long way from that first flight. Every system I build today — every line of code — carries part of this story with it.
