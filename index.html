My Journey

The long road that shaped me.

Not everything is on the resume. Here's the story that got me here.

The Dream Begins

I didn’t grow up planning to study abroad. The idea arrived quietly and stayed. I wanted something harder. Something that would push me beyond what felt comfortable. In a family where my siblings were accomplished doctors, I knew I had to carve my own space.

Engineering felt natural. I liked breaking things down, understanding how systems worked, and building something from nothing. Still, the question followed me everywhere: Why not become a doctor?

“Every time I answer that question, I pause. I’ve doubted it before — but this path felt honest.”

Flight Toward the Dream

I started preparing in my third year. The exams themselves were manageable — what I never anticipated was the emotional cost of staying committed to a goal that looked different from everyone else’s.

During that time, campus placements were happening. Friends were interviewing, getting offers, celebrating. While they were moving forward, I was choosing to wait. That quiet discipline — staying focused amid all that noise — was harder than any exam.

I cleared the qualifying exams. Applications went out. Rejections came first — then one day, an email arrived. My first admit letter. I was sitting with friends when I saw it. Everyone noticed. Everyone celebrated. For the first time, the dream felt real.

I thought the hard part was over. Then COVID hit. My 2020 plans collapsed overnight. Everything was deferred. The waiting stretched on — filled with uncertainty, paperwork, and anxiety. Somewhere in that chaos, I injured my hand. Pain became background noise.

And then came the day I left home. I still remember the faces. The silence. The weight of it all. I had never flown before — not even once. My first flight wasn’t for vacation — it was to the U.S. Embassy in Delhi. That’s when it truly sank in: this was happening.

“Three bags. Twenty-three years. One flight.”

New Country, New Pressure

The U.S. felt overwhelming at first. New food. New weather. Speaking English all day took effort. I overloaded credits, worked as a TA, and tried to save money wherever I could.

Everyone talked about internships. I didn’t get one. Summer came — and I stayed back, alone in the dorms.

Then came the final semester — and still no interviews. I knew it was going to be a long battle. I picked up a night shift job at the library so I could save money, attend classes during the day, and apply for jobs in between.

“I was always tired — but I kept going. I had to.”

Borrowed Time

Graduation came with no job offer — only a ticking clock. I had 90 days. When I narrowly missed a required grade in my ML specialization, I thought another door had closed.

Instead, a professor offered me a Research Assistant role. It didn’t solve everything — but it gave me what I needed most: time.

“That one offer gave me time. And I needed all of it.”

Survival Mode

Money ran out. Rejections kept coming. I picked up my bike and went door to door — shop to shop — looking for part-time work. Nothing.

Then a break — my roommate’s old gas station job needed help. I said yes. It was winter. No Uber. No buses. I biked two miles in the cold every day. Stocked shelves. Cleaned floors. Took out trash. Just one more month — that was the goal.

After that, I got a job at Subway. The owner was around my sister’s age. She became like a big sister. One of the other workers — a funny Indian lady — made me laugh during shifts. For the first time in a long while, I didn’t feel completely alone.

“One month of survival. That was the goal.”

The Call

By now, it had been ten months since graduation — ten months of rejection emails, sleepless nights, cold meals, cold rides, and learning to expect nothing... and still trying anyway.

Then came October. A message popped up: interview opportunity. Broadridge. I didn’t react. I’d learned not to. Just another chance — nothing more.

I squeezed in prep between Subway shifts. Still cooking all my meals. No Uber. No takeout. Just persistence — and a laptop that never went cold.

First round: managerial. Calm. Focused. Curious. They liked me. Second round: coding. I got stuck. My heart sank. But I locked in. Not with fresh prep — but with the muscle memory of long, lonely nights. I asked for a hint. I solved it.

An hour later, my phone rang.

“We’d like to offer you the job.”

The Breakthrough

I cried — not because of the job, but because of everything it took to get there. Not quiet tears — but the kind that come when your body finally lets go.

From gas station floors. From taped fingers. From missed opportunities. From pride. From silence. From stubborn hope.

It wasn’t luck. It was persistence.

“If you keep going — even when it’s quiet, even when it hurts — you’ll make it.”
